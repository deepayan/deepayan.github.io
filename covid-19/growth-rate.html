* Trying to measure growth rate rather than absolute number of cases * This is related to [this ad hoc measure](increment-ratio.html), but more grounded in theory * Motivation: - Let $x(t)$ be total number infected at time $t$ - Let $a(t)$ be number of active infections at time $t$ - Let $\lambda(t)$ be the _number of new infections per active infection per unit time_ at time $t$ - Then a reasonable way to model the evolution of $x(t)$ is $$ x(t + \delta t) - x(t) \approx \delta t \, a(t) \, \lambda(t) $$ --- - This gives an instantaneous definition of $\lambda(t)$ as $$ \lambda(t) = \frac{ \frac{dx(t)}{dt} }{ a(t) } \approx \frac{ \delta x(t) }{ \delta t \, a(t) } $$ - We do have daily data, but using $\delta t = 1$ without smoothing gives very noisy results - We instead use $\delta t = 7$ - In theory, this measure can be compared across time and location - It should not depend on degree of systematic under-reporting - It should not be overly affected by daily fluctuations and patterns - We report $R(t) := 10 * \lambda(t)$ assuming each active person is infectious for 10 days - Any $R(t)$ higher than 1 is bad on a sustained basis - What is a threhold for it to be _really_ bad? --- - Calculations based on data from [covid19india.org](https://covid19india.org) - $x(t) = \text{ Confirmed}$ - $a(t) = x(t) - \text{ Recovered } - \text{ Deceased }$ .center[ [ Updated: 2021-04-27 ] ] --- layout: false #
